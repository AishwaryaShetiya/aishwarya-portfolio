Oasis: Where AI Meets
Real Users

An agentic browser that doesn't just chat beside your tabs. It plans, searches, and acts on them. I led product management and designed the feedback loop that made the AI smarter with every correction.

AI Product Manager · Kahana Group · Sep 2025 to Apr 2026
The product now serves 7,000+ users across 108 countries.
See it live ↗ Deep dive ↗
What Is Oasis
Oasis is an AI browser where the assistant is grounded in your actual tabs, history, and the page you're currently on. Not a chatbot bolted into a sidebar. It can find what you half-remember, summarize what you're reading, and act on your tabs through plain-language commands. It's built privacy-first: anonymized by default, with no user identity attached unless you choose to add it.
The Problem
Knowledge workers lose hours to tab chaos. No structure, no context, constant context-switching. Existing browsers are passive. They show you what's open but never help you understand what matters. The context lives in your head, not the tool. Intelligent hub grouping was the feature built to close that gap.
My Role
AI Product Manager · Kahana Group · Sep 2025 to Apr 2026
I wrote the full PRD for intelligent hub grouping, the core feature that lets Oasis automatically organize tabs by project context. That meant going deep on how users actually navigate: when do they context-switch? When does AI-suggested grouping feel helpful vs. annoying?

I built the telemetry to observe how users reacted to AI-driven groupings, sat with them as they thought out loud, and turned every override into a learning signal. The AI suggested. The user corrected. The product got smarter.
How It Works / What I Learned
Trust is won at the confirmation
We made the AI confirm before closing a tab, showing the exact command in plain language first. A small design decision, but giving an AI permission to act on your browser is precisely where trust is won or lost. Get that moment wrong and users never hand the AI anything that matters.
Privacy as architecture, not a setting
Privacy wasn't bolted on at the end. The default data payload carries no user ID. The assistant works without knowing who you are unless you choose to tell it. Building a feedback loop that learns from corrections without tying them to an identity is harder, and more interesting, than the version that just logs everything.
The override is the signal
Most products treat user disagreement as friction to remove. We treated it as the most valuable signal we had. Every time someone moved a tab out of an AI-suggested group, that correction told us something the model couldn't learn on its own. The product that listens to its corrections beats the product that never makes them.

Job Search Automation
Project · AI Automation

Job Search
Automation

I built a multi-workflow system that scrapes job postings, scores them for fit, finds the right people to reach out to, and drafts tailored outreach and application materials — all before I wake up. It's a working example of how I think: break a messy problem into coordinated stages, use AI as a build partner, and keep the human in control of every decision that matters.

The Problem
Job searching is repetitive, high-volume, and low-signal. The work that actually matters — deciding where to apply, who to reach out to, what to say — gets buried under hours of manual scraping, copy-pasting, and resume edits. I wanted to automate the busywork and keep my attention on the decisions.
The Approach
I started with one question: if I had a magic wand, what would the system do for me? Find relevant jobs, score them, tell me who to talk to, draft the outreach, and deliver it as a morning briefing. Each capability became its own workflow, passing data to the next. I built it in n8n, with AI as my architect and debugger — no engineering background required.
The Stack
n8nAutomation engine that runs and connects every workflow.
ApifyScrapes LinkedIn job postings by keyword, so the search is as wide as my keywords, not a fixed company list.
NotionThe databases (Jobs, Companies, Contacts, Materials) that hold state across workflows.
Google DocsStores my master resume and holds the working drafts the system reads from and writes back to.
ChatGPTDrafts outreach and generates a tailored resume in Overleaf LaTeX plus a matching cover letter for each role.
Claude (Cowork)Researches target companies and surfaces relevant new people to reach out to.
GmailDelivers the briefing to my inbox.
Two AI tools, each for what it's best at — ChatGPT for structured generation, Claude for open-ended research.
The System, Workflow by Workflow
Each workflow runs on a schedule and passes its output to the next.
Workflow 1
Auto-fetch Jobs
Runs daily. Apify scrapes LinkedIn job postings by keyword, then the workflow cleans and structures the raw results, loops through each posting, checks the Jobs database to skip duplicates, applies exclusion rules for the wrong title or seniority, and saves only genuinely new, relevant roles. A rate-limit delay in the loop keeps the scraper from overloading the API. This is the workflow where I debugged a 403 authentication error and a context- and credit-limit issue — solving those taught me more about the system than building the happy path did.
Workflow 2
Score Jobs
Pulls each new, unscored job and calculates a fit score weighing role tier, salary, remote status, and whether I have a path into the company. Scores are written back to Notion so the best-fit roles rise to the top. This is the layer that turns a flat list into a ranked queue, so my attention goes to the strongest matches instead of everything.
Workflow 3
Find & Match the Right People
For each role, the system identifies who to reach out to. If I already know someone at the company, it surfaces that contact for a warm message. If I don't, I use Claude Cowork to research the company and surface relevant new people worth connecting with. It turns "who do I know here?" into "who should I talk to here?" — through someone I know or someone new.
Workflow 4
Generate Outreach & Application Materials
Branches by situation. For someone I know, it drafts a warm message that uses the relationship. For a new person, it drafts a cold LinkedIn connection request with a personalized note. For the application itself, I keep a master resume in Google Docs as the single source of truth; the system pairs it with the job description and uses ChatGPT to produce a tailored resume that surfaces the most relevant experience — returned as Overleaf LaTeX, ready to compile — along with a matching cover letter. Every output is a draft I review before anything is sent or submitted.
Workflow 5
Morning Briefing
Pulls the day's new, scored jobs, orders them by fit, and writes a plain-language summary of the best matches. It delivers that briefing straight to my inbox each morning, so I wake up to a ranked shortlist of the strongest new roles instead of an empty search bar.
Workflow 6
Error Notification
A safety net. If any workflow fails, this one catches the error and emails me immediately, so a silent failure never costs me a day. A system you can't trust to tell you when it broke isn't one you can rely on.
Human-AI Co-Design
I built this by working with AI as architect, debugger, and reasoning partner — plan, build one step at a time, test until something breaks, debug, repeat. The most useful reframe of the whole project: failure isn't rejection, it's data. Every broken run taught me how the system actually worked.
What I Kept Human
The system handles the mechanical part — fetching, organizing, scoring, finding people, drafting. I keep the part that affects other people: the decision to reach out or apply, and a review of every message and every resume before it goes out. The system generates drafts, never finished actions. For a search built on relationships, that boundary isn't a limitation — it's the point.
Impact
Under 10 min
Per-Application Tailoring (from 20–25)
What used to take 20–25 minutes of manual tailoring per application now takes under 10 — including reviewing every draft, evaluating the fit, and making sure it's genuinely personalized to me and the role.
Reflection
What I really built isn't a job-search tool. It's proof of how I work: take an overwhelming, ambiguous problem, break it into coordinated stages, use AI as a force multiplier, design for the human to stay in control, and keep iterating after v1. That's the same instinct I bring to product.
Built with n8n, Apify, ChatGPT, and Claude. Iterating on v2 now.
